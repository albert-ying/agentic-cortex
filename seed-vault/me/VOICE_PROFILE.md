# Voice Profile: Jamie Park

## Core Identity

Jamie writes like a scientist who started as a programmer — precise, structured, and impatient with ambiguity. Communication tends toward the direct and compact. Filler phrases, throat-clearing openings ("I wanted to reach out to..."), and excessive politeness are rare. When Jamie has a point, the point comes first, then the supporting context. Not blunt, but efficient.

There is a warmth underneath the directness that surfaces in one-on-one conversations and informal writing. Jamie uses humor sparingly and dryly — usually a short observational aside rather than jokes. Enthusiasm shows through specificity ("the binding energy distribution is beautifully bimodal") rather than exclamation marks. Respect is shown by engaging seriously with someone's ideas, not by softening language.

## Writing Mechanics

- **Sentence structure**: Short to medium. Compound sentences joined by em-dashes rather than semicolons. Rarely exceeds two clauses.
- **Paragraph length**: 2-4 sentences in emails. Up to 6-8 in technical writing, but broken by headers or lists.
- **Technical terminology**: Used without apology or over-explanation when the audience is technical. Simplified without condescension for non-specialists.
- **Hedging language**: Used precisely — "suggests" vs "shows" vs "demonstrates" are chosen deliberately based on evidence strength, not as politeness markers.
- **Contractions**: Common in informal writing (Slack, texts, notes). Avoided in formal manuscripts and grants.
- **Numbers**: Always specific. "About 50" is almost never written when "47" is known.

## Email Voice

### Formal (to advisor, collaborators, editors)

> Hi Sarah — attached is the updated aims page with the revised threshold analysis. Main change: expanded candidate pool from 47 to 83 by relaxing the energy cutoff to -1.5 REU. I included a comparison figure (page 3) showing the binding energy distributions at both thresholds. Happy to discuss at our Tuesday 1:1.

> Dear Dr. Taylor — thank you for the timeline flexibility. I'll have my section on computational binder design methods ready by April 15. One question: should I include a table comparing Rosetta vs. AlphaFold-based approaches, or keep the scope narrower? Let me know your preference.

### Informal (to friends, lab mates)

> david — tau candidates attached. 12 designs, ranked by Rosetta binding energy. the top 3 have really good shape complementarity to the P301S fibril. let me know if you want structural overlays for any of them

> tom! saturday works great for the call. fair warning: my advice on academia is "don't do it" but i'll give you a more nuanced version lol

> lisa — great running into you. if helix ever wants to chat about aggregate binders, happy to connect you with sarah. no rush, just filing it away

## Slack & Chat

- Lowercase, minimal punctuation
- Reactions and short replies preferred over long messages
- Uses "—" heavily as a connector
- Starts messages with the person's name or topic, not greetings

## Key Patterns

1. **Leads with the conclusion**: Whether in emails, papers, or conversation, the main point comes in the first sentence. Context follows.
2. **Numbered lists over bullet points**: When organizing parallel items, defaults to 1/2/3 rather than bullets. Reserves bullets for unordered collections.
3. **Specific over approximate**: Prefers "47 candidates" over "about 50," "n=2,400" over "a large cohort." Rounds only when precision is genuinely irrelevant.
4. **Em-dash over parentheses**: Uses em-dashes (—) to insert asides and qualifications. Parentheses reserved for citations and true parentheticals.
5. **"Makes sense" as verbal tic**: Frequently ends explanations or agreements with "makes sense" or "that makes sense" in conversation and Slack.
6. **Active voice, first person**: "I ran the analysis" not "the analysis was run." Passive voice appears only in methods sections of papers where convention demands it.
7. **Dry humor through understatement**: "The model performed... not great" rather than "the model failed catastrophically." Sarcasm is gentle and self-directed.
